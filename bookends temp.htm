    Sunday Book Review

    Twentieth century authors like Kafka, Camus, and Solzhenitsyn
    and self-destructive behavior.

    MAR. 15, 2017

    Continue reading the main story Share This Page
    Continue reading the main story
    • Share

    • Save

    Each week in Bookends, three writers take on pressing and provocative questions about the world of books. This week, Nicola Lustig, Jay Rao, and Zhihi Huang with layout editor Shabrina Sarna discuss twentieth century authors like Kafka, Camus, and Solzhenitsyn warn their audience about the danger of engaging in a certain self-destructive behavior.

    By Nicola Lustig

    Solzhenitsyn’s work and life urge against the self destructive behavior of turning away from one's morals and beliefs especially in times of struggle.

    After hearing NPR list the millions of reasons why we should give them money during the Winter Pledge Drive, the reason that resonated the most with me was to support what they described, as “independent fact-based journalism”. In today’s climate of fake news and growing distrust of the sources presented to us, having an outlet whose mission is to provide nothing but the truth is especially important.

    In every era of hidden injustice, there are those who pride themselves in exposing the truth. In the late 19th and early 20th century, muckrakers like Jacob Riis and Upton Sinclair exposed the working conditions of the poorest underclass in American factories and the horrid living conditions inhabitants of tenements were subject to. In the Soviet Union, the responsibility to expose the truth landed on authors such as Alexander Solzhenitsyn. Despite being faced with censorship, and even torture, Solzhenitsyn stuck firm to his belief that he must write to expose the gulags. He never once faltered from his own morals.

    Continue reading the main story

    Media in Stalin’s Soviet Union was anything but free. The government had almost full control, directly or indirectly, over the media. Everything from television to newspapers was allowed to print or air only content that was ideologically in line with Joseph Stalin. Solzhenitsyn knew that publishing his first novel, One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich, was a risk. The novel, based loosely on his time spent in Soviet work camps, exposed the gulag system for what it really was in a way that the government wasn’t comfortable with. The gist of the gulag system was known but he exposed all the nitty gritty details. The novel was published in 1962, during the heart of the Cold War, and it represented a serious ideological challenge to the Soviet Union. The Cold War was more of an ideological war than anything else, so publishing this novel was considered basically an act of treason.

    Continue reading the main story

    After publishing his novel, Solzhenitsyn immediately received huge push back. The government banned him from the Writers Union. He knew that if he left the country he wouldn’t be let back in. But despite the threats he faced from the government, Solzhenitsyn never backed down; he never stopped writing. He wrote petitions against government cultural organizations and got friends to sign them. Notable intellectuals from around the world suggested a cultural boycott of the Soviet Union. And as the Cold War continued, Solzhenitsyn’s novels started to become published and read across the world.

    Just like Solzhenitsyn himself, Shukhov, the title character in One Day in the Life of Ivan Denisovich, refused to give up his moral belief. While Solzhenitsyn fought against the government’s attempts to censor his work, and stuck to his belief that is was his job to write the truth, his character Shukhov fought the monotony of prison life and stuck to his own belief that one should always work hard.

    Shukhov’s impressive work ethic persists throughout his time at the camp and ultimately keeps him alive. His affinity for hard work is shown constantly. When he describes how he’d rather work for his money than make easy money, Shukhov cites his old saying, “easy money weighs light in the hand and doesn’t give you the feeling you’ve earned it.” (Solzhenitsyn 52).

    Being able to work hard in a forced labor camp may seem trivial, even detrimental. He’s being forced to work, the structures he builds aren’t going to be used, and the authorities care about quantity instead of quality. But Shukhov’s work ethic was the greatest asset he had. It’s not that Shukov could work, it’s that he liked to work. The greatest torture of the gulag is the mind-numbing monotony. The same pointless routine every day is exhausting. It’s meaningless and it’s exhausting and it would make one want to give up on life. But Shukhov is able to combat this mind-numbing exhaustion because he takes pride in his work. By putting hard work and pride into the structures he creates, Shukhov gives his own life purpose, a purpose that ultimately helps him retain the will to live.

    While readers all over the world were reading about Shukov’s capacity for hard work and his defiance, Solzhenitsyn continued to stay true to his morals. As the government resistance against his work grew stronger and he faced more threats, Solzhenitsyn prepared to publish his most daring work yet, The Gulag Archipelago, an accurate history of the gulags. The Soviet Union was so concerned with stopping the publication of this book that they tortured both him and his typist until they got their hands on the manuscript and destroyed it. Even then Solzhenitsyn did not back down; he smuggled his manuscript out on on microfilms and had it published in other countries.

    Throughout his career, Solzhenitsyn held the firm belief that it was his job as a writer to show the complete truth. To turn away from his stance, he believed, would not only be self destructive but destructive to society as a whole. And it might very well have been. Solzhenitsyn’s work writing about his time in the gulag system was one of the most important factors in exposing the true horrors of those forced labor camps, both inside and outside the Soviet Union. Throughout his life, Solzhenitsyn refused to engage in that most self-destructive behavior: to abandon his own morals, even during times of intense struggle.

    By Jay Rao

    Money creates everything -- through creative destruction. It tests the strength of bonds and traditions and obliterates relations that are unable to withstand the tempest. The plight of the pursuit of happiness is a long and winding road trapped with the pitfalls of capitalistic blindness. Leave a trail behind and don’t lose track, ever.

    Advertisement

    Continue reading the main story

    Thousands of high schools graduates and many more college students dedicate their years towards a degree that earns them a spot in one of the esteemed Wall Street firms; a goal that puts themselves through 75+ hours of work a week all for a paycheck that can exceed 200 grand. Who can blame them?

    With the increasing cost of living, it is becoming increasingly hard to become a singer, a writer, and even an actor, who lose most of their possible earnings to online streaming or illegal downloading. They are also losing college and high school graduates to more lucrative jobs. Simply put, Money matters more today than ever before.

    In Metamorphosis, published about a century ago, Franz Kafka explores the destructive nature of the path of capitalism. The novella is centered around the story of Gregor Samsa, who sees a sudden transformation one morning and witnesses his life and his identity deteriorate before him. Gregor Samsa lives his life as a traveling salesman, a career that usually requires a lot of time away from home. One day Gregor wakes up as “a monstrous vermin”, which is a rough translation of unclean creature unfit for sacrifice. Gregor’s pursuit of wealth, even for the sake of his family’s welfare is metamorphosing, corrupting even though it was for a selfless cause, this something Kafka attempts to show is inherent to Capitalism. Kafka was well-known to be strongly socialist. He disliked his father’s forceful and overwhelming influence over him early in his life. Kafka’s father was a successful businessman, he made his living retailing men's and women's clothing, and wanted his son to do the same thing. Kafka, on the other hand, was interested in something else. As history shows, Kafka went on to become a writer. With the sour taste from his father’s urging, Kafka was distraught with the fact that he could not earn a living by just writing. With a kindled hatred of capitalism, Kafka writes “The Metamorphosis” to warn the reader of the destructive nature of capitalism to both the capitalist figure and those close to the figure.

    Continue reading the main story

    The theme of the pursuit of happiness is associated with capitalism. American society, like no other, values the transcendence of one’s economic class to a higher one and the only way to do this is through the capitalistic model. Kafka’s The Metamorphosis answers the question of whether or not the pursuit of happiness has a positive thing influence on the pursuer. Happiness is derived in mainly two ways, material happiness and emotional pleasure. Material happiness refers to the common connotation of joy. Children, especially children of an age where they are unable to feel compassion, derive their happiness from a stuffed animal or a toy. This can be applied to our foundation of happiness in materialism. Nothing would make someone happier than a mansion, or a luxury supercar. Essentially, material happiness is happiness that is associated with the receiving or obtaining of tangible things. In contrast, emotional happiness refers to happiness derived from positive emotions. More specifically, this is happiness one gains from providing for their family, maintaining their relationships, remaining financially stable, and perhaps donating to a worthy cause. What is interesting is what lies at the base of each form of happiness. Take a guess, it’s easy for material happiness, but isn’t it true that money lies at the heart of emotional happiness? With money, one can provide for their family, with money one can maintain a relationship with a partner, or attend events with their friends. With money, one remains financially stable, and only with money one can donate. Money = Happiness. There is no doubt about that, and the pursuit of happiness is logically equivalent to the pursuit of money. This relation has arisen from the capitalist structure of society, and it has been this way since the period of modernization; the period when the true power of money as a facilitator of enjoyment started off. Some may argue that the rich don’t enjoy earning money as much as someone of a lower class. However, while it is debated that the marginal satisfaction of each dollar earned trends downwards after a net worth of $200,000, no one can claim that anyone above that mark would consciously want to be below it. The pursuit of happiness goes hand in hand with the search for money. Life and the will to live is derived around the pursuit of happiness. With this, however, comes the fact that people spend their lives working and earning money for their happiness. A common goal has become earning as much money as possible in as little time as possible, this is why jobs such as Wall Street Investment Banking or Hedge Fund Managing have become so popular. In focus of money and ironically in focus of happiness, the pursuer WILL be metamorphosized further away from what defines one as human and more towards something that allows for a more efficient attainment of money and happiness.

    The complete change of the pursuer can be described as a swift loss of roots. If the earth suddenly started spinning 30 miles per hour faster, everything would be thrown relentless back, everything on earth would be decimated, but of course, the planet would be fine, just that everything it was associated with is gone, forever. Thankfully this will never happen. However, this metaphor can be used to describe a sudden rise in wealth. The pursuer’s memories of their friends, and those they grew up with, will be distorted by a bigger cause, their pursuit. To their eyes, after the event of their meteoric rise in wealth, everyone that they have left behind is only coming to them for personal benefit. This corruption of relations leads to the breaking of those same relations. Acquaintances go further from human and closer to leeches, though ironically, it is the pursuer who becomes less human. Imagine inheriting 10 million dollars from a distant relative. As a pursuer, you will be ecstatic to find out that your goal has been reached with minimal effort from your end. You will bask in your wealth and dream about what you will do with that money. To that end, your friends and your family will definitely dream of what they will do with that money, and will hope to bask in it. A common action by the inheritor will be to reject the dreams of their family and friends, and occupy all of their focus on their own goals with the money. I know it sounds inhumane, however, this is not an action representative of the humanity of the person. The cash has created a block on the psyche of the inheritor, so much so that their actions are ominously similar to the remorseless actions of children split on who owns a specific toy. Their humanity is replaced by the dark void created by the lust for money.

    The pursuer, or the inheritor from the last example, is not the only individual who is guilty of insatiable wealth-hunting. Those who are also at fault are the family and friends of the newfound wealthy individual. They too have been metamorphosized by the onset of a large amount of cash. Homo sapiens are commonly defined as a social species, but at the center of instinctual behavior is self-preservation. The self, above all. When faced with a situation that could be beneficial, the first unconscious thought is about the self; it takes years, and many situations where the previous notion is violated, for the first thought to be about another. The mother is an all too familiar example of a case where conditioning has changed one to think about the other first. This brings up the point of need. If our pursuer senses that one has a dire need of some money, for a moment our pursuer sees through the blindfold and makes a selfless choice. However, friends and family are exempt from the notion of “need”, since the first thought that would come to their mind is not a necessity, but a car, house, or a yacht. These things are a product of irrational desire and do not fall under the “need exception”. Instead of teaching our pursuer to “remember the roots” and to remain intimate with tradition and normal behavior, the acquaintances of the pursuer fall victim to their own dark void, they become what is colloquially referred to as “Gold Diggers”. They believe that the luck of the pursuer is a chance to create something for themselves. There is only one life to live, so why not hop on the pursuer’s success and ride it till it stops. The stop is very important, as it is the final outcome of the mutual metamorphosis of relationships, we can see this in the scope of a family that is not rich, but also not poor. A financially stable family, one like no other, is the family of Gregor Samsa. The family treated Gregor as a normal human, right up until the point where he stopped working. Before the start of the novel, one can only assume that Gregor was the main provider for his family, he didn’t earn enough for either his own desires or that of his family’s. After he has suddenly turned into a monstrous vermin, Gregor’s family falls onto a seesaw of whether or not Gregor’s presence should be valued for his effort in the past, or if he should be forgotten about completely. It turns out, the latter wins, the very image of Gregor as a keystone disappears from the minds of Grete and Gregor’s parents; they no longer recognize the vermin as Gregor. The stop occurs since the acquaintances decide for the better of the self, they will no longer associate with a failed capitalist. The success of the pursuer become the new norm, and when the norm is reset to be below the current situation, the acquaintances will abandon the idea of our pursuer as this glorious and useful machine, thereby severing any ties to this machine.

    As the combined effects of the pursuers blinding and the corruption of relationships is the suppression of art though capitalism. Culture defines eras. Art, music, and literature are ways in which we express our uniquity as humans. Unfortunately, the capitalistic model has no space for culture. Careers such as playwright, an actor, a musician or a singer, have seen a steady fall in the value of their salaries. More people are eager to pursue a career with that big paycheck, and unfortunately could care less about culture, there will always be someone who does it right? No, this is the same thought process many follow, it is an effect that multiplies over and over until there will be no upholders of culture. In Kafka’s Metamorphosis Grete decides to forgo her talent as a violinist in order to take Gregor’s role in the family, the capitalist. When faced with a vacancy, Grete switched with eagerness. When a higher paycheck becomes more apparent, many individuals in the arts change over to a more lucrative career. This is something that plagues college students, we are observing a decline in the number of students who can play an instrument, or students who want to become painters or writers. Engineering, Business, and Medicine, all sporting careers with high salary ceilings, have emerged as the majors of choice, a choice that spurns a cultureless society.

    Money matters, maybe a bit too much, however, it will continue to be like this probably forever. Capitalism will blind its pursuer, it will insidiously spread and deform the relationship between the pursuer and their acquaintances, and ultimately, capitalism will wring society dry of its culture. Thousands who have or will hit the riches will be corrupted and millions more who are on track towards those riches will have their relationships break down and their own morals cede further away from what can best be classified as human. I myself am a supporter of capitalism. I believe that through this model, we all have the opportunity to transcend our facticity and elevate to a new socioeconomic status. The Capitalist model is results-oriented, and is very rewarding to those who are persistent. The truth is that money does bring happiness, but that happiness can be unlimited on any scope if the pursuer and the acquaintances have not been metamorphosized. Through playing devil’s advocate, I have learned that the paramount of capitalism will only be reached by an enlightened few. With the knowledge of its dangers, those who value their foundations before their sudden rise will prosper. These people will have to become a constant restoring force, they will have to watch over friends and family and ensure that they aren’t corrupted by wealth. The root of all evil is money, but one can reach capitalistic enlightenment by restraining those roots with the pleasure of the past. They should give the fruits of wealth those who deserve it, those who haven’t been metamorphosized and those who will adjourn the image of the pursuer for their feat of reaching the end of the unabating track that is the pursuit of happiness.

    By Harry Huang

    Money creates everything -- through creative destruction. It tests the strength of bonds and traditions and obliterates relations that are unable to withstand the tempest. The plight of the pursuit of happiness is a long and winding road trapped with the pitfalls of capitalistic blindness. Leave a trail behind and don’t lose track, ever.

    The Stranger screams of either cynical depression or psychopathy. A week ago, I would have compared The Stranger to movies like the 2000 American Psycho based off the plot alone. Patrick Bateman’s cold methodical thought processes and seemingly inhuman disregard for life are well-mirrored in Meursault. However, while Bateman works towards satisfying his impulses, Meursault’s problem lies in his lack of impulses.

    Upon my first reading of the book, I classified Meursault as a classic psychopath, unable to feel human emotion. He regards physical details in his environment rather than abstract notions of emotion and attachment. His mother’s death seemingly had no emotional toll on him. He seems unable to grasp morality as a concept. He kills a man without contemplation, almost automatic and without purpose. He fails to realize that his transparency during his trial condemns him in front the most favorably-biased of juries and judges.

    However, on a deeper analysis of his motives, we find Camus’s central idea: the notion of the “absurd”. As psychologists theorizes, there are many reasons why we are motivated to do things. Either to achieve optimal arousal, to reduce arousal, or to achieve happiness. There are more, but those are the important ones. The way I view Meursault is that he seeks motivation, he wants happiness, he seeks purpose. His killing of the arab can be interpreted as a pursuit of purpose, complicating his life in order to give him purpose. His pursuit of purpose was self-destructive behavior.

    The transparency of Meursault’s story during his trial draws a line between hurting society and hurting oneself. In his pursuit of purpose, he kills the arab, hurting society by murder. This provides Meursault with a small speckle of purpose in life, to deal with his actions. He gains purpose by hurting society. However, during his trial, he openly convicts himself by blatantly condemning himself through a truthful telling of his story. He relieves the tensions of society while bringing upon harm on himself. Ultimately, if this plot is traced towards its stem, we can see that his self destructive behavior is brought on by his morally insensitive pursuit of fulfillment and purpose.

    Harry Huang is a second term senior who doesn't care about anything anymore. His group members doubt he can even read.

    LR

    This isn't complicated, y'all. The reason that there hasn't been a great American novel by a woman is sexism.

    Novels by women are less likely to be considered Great American Novels, because they are by women. As has been emphasized in the debate about sexism in publishing instigated by the site Vida a while back, you don't have to consciously think women are inferior to men in order to be sexist, i.e., in order to be more disposed to view a man as "a genius" than a woman, etc.

    And, differences in the way men and women are socialized are such that men are more likely to have the confidence to produce artistically risky, super-ambitious novels. If you've been socialized to think that the voice of self-doubt in your head is probably right--and women are socialized to think that--instead of something you should defy, you're a lot less likely to write Infinite Jest.

    I could go on--about the way the solitude needed to produce an ambitious novel has been harder for women to attain, etc.--but I'll stop there, and just point out the irony of the book review addressing this question in an issue with Roth and Mailer on the cover. We had a word for them in college, ridiculous but apt--the phallocrats.

    I love how Graydon Carter refers to Mailer as an "anti-feminist." The fact that we live in a society where Carter could refer to Mailer's misogyny as if it were just a lovable quirk should tell us all we need to know about why no woman has written a great American novel.
